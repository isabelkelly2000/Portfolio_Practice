Designed for Decision: Executive Information Design at Airbnb

At Airbnb, major product launches and strategic initiatives are reviewed at the highest level. I design the information that makes the details clear enough to discuss, react to, and build from.

ABOUT THIS PROJECT

Project Summary

At Airbnb, major product launches, partnerships, and strategic initiatives are reviewed at the highest level — by CEO Brian Chesky and members of the E-Team across Marketing, Comms, Product, and Supply. Some of these reviews require a combination of a presentation on screen with a series of large-format printed boards displayed throughout the room. The deck drives the narrative. The boards are where the details live: timelines, press coverage, supply data, strategy, and visual evidence of the work leading up to a launch.

As the founding member of Airbnb's executive review board practice, I partner with teams across Airbnb to translate complex information into large-format artifacts designed for quick comprehension and discussion. I also developed and actively maintain the standardized board template system used across executive reviews — establishing the typographic hierarchy, layout structure, and print standards that bring consistency to the process across teams.

THE PROBLEM

The Challenge

Executive reviews require everything a team knows about a launch — timelines, strategy, data, press, supply — to be absorbed in one place, at scale, in real time. The work has to land from across the room and still hold up when someone walks up to read the fine print.

Most teams arrive with deep knowledge of their initiative but no experience translating it into large-format communication. Spatial hierarchy, content density, and print production are an entirely different discipline. The boards are printed once, the meeting happens once, and the work has to be right.

The challenge is not just what to show — it's how to make complex, varied information immediately legible in an environment built for discussion, scrutiny, and rapid decision-making.

THE WORK

Solution

The nature of executive reviews means the work is never truly finished until the initiative is. Boards evolve alongside the strategy — feedback from one review reshapes the next, and the design has to move just as fast as the decisions being made. In many cases, the challenge is not simply presenting finalized information, but helping teams clarify what they are actually trying to communicate in the first place.

Rather than treating boards as isolated deliverables, the process was designed as an iterative system: collaborative, scalable, and built to support rapid revision across multiple review cycles. Sometimes boards need to change at the drop of a hat, and I make sure it can happen.

Deliverable 1: Board Template System

I created a standardized design system for large-format printed boards at Airbnb. This included a defined Figma frame size that accurately represents physical board dimensions, a consistent header and title treatment, and a typographic hierarchy that holds up at scale. It also introduced designing for print entirely within Figma as a repeatable workflow. Over time, I built out a repository of completed boards that performed well in CEO reviews — a reference library of layouts and structures that new projects can pull from.

It starts with a conversation. Before anything is designed, time is spent understanding what a team is trying to communicate, how information should be divided across boards, what each board needs to accomplish individually, and how they all come together holistically.

The first round of boards is built in close contact with POCs across the team. This stage is less about visual polish and more about establishing a healthy foundation — getting deeply familiar with the information, what exists, what it means, and how it fits into the larger story the team is trying to tell. The design stays close to Airbnb's existing visual language, adapting their design system to the unique demands of large-format print.

Once draft one is complete, a Source of Truth Figma file is shared with the team as the central working document. From there, the process becomes collaborative — teams contribute edits and updates directly as the boards evolve, while I stay close to the file to ensure spacing, hierarchy, and cleanliness are maintained throughout.

Before the boards reach the CEO, they go through a review with team leads, VPs, and directors. Depending on the seniority of who is reviewing, this happens virtually or in person with printed boards. This stage is where the work gets pressure tested. Sometimes these reviews refine details, and other times they completely reframe how the story is being told. Leadership direction can shift overnight, and when it does the boards have to move with it, whether that means small adjustments or starting a section from scratch.

Printing is its own layer of the design process. Airbnb's design standard is Figma — a tool built for digital, not print — so producing large-format output at this quality required developing a reliable export process from the ground up. Regardless of what assets come in — low-resolution screenshots, compressed imagery, inconsistent source materials — the end result has to be scaled, pixel perfect, and print ready. Final prints range anywhere from 4 to 40 feet, making clarity and visual quality at physical scale critical to the final outcome.

Once the boards are in the room, the work is out of my hands — but rarely finished. Brian's feedback is interpreted through the team and translated back into a design brief, and the process starts again. Each iteration brings the work closer to a final version, until the initiative lands or the review cycle closes.
